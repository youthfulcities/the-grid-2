Pivot 2020 Index Development Process

The Pivot 2020 Index measures cities’ youthfulness by looking at the way people live, work, play and thrive in their city. This allows the project to identify the city's strengths and areas of improvement for building better cities. Since 2013, Youthful Cities has created 6 unique indexes involving more than 75 cities from across the globe covering topics and creating indicators that are important to youth. Including an index as one of the three research tools complemented Pivot 2020’s project goals, so Youthful Cities offered to use its index as a base from which to build.


Building the Pivot Index

Pivot 2020 wanted to update the Youthful Cities Index indicators to ensure that they best captured the lived experiences of youth in cities across Canada in accessible, inclusive, equitable, and diverse ways. As Pivot 2020 continues to strive for inclusion and validating the expertise of youth, the project invited youth representatives from youth-led and youth-serving organizations across the country to join an advisory committee and network.

The Index EDI review process magnified the voices of Queer, Trans, Black, Indigenous, and People of Colour from regions across the country. The representatives from the youth-led and youth-serving organizations, convened for 8 weeks virtually, to discuss and workshop indicators for an index that better captures the diverse experiences and realities of youth across the country. The Youthful Cities Index measures what it’s like for youth to live, work, play and thrive in their city. While the youth-led EDI process was happening, there was a concurrent internal review by Pivot 2020’s Equity Lead and Partnerships Director.

The outcome of the Index EDI review process informed the adaptation for the Youthful Cities Index for Pivot 2020. For Pivot 2020, the index grew from 21 topics to 23 (some became amalgamated and others completely replaced) and now consists of over 400 indicators.


Youth-led & Youth Serving Organizations

Youthful Cities asked that organizations prioritize those who may not have had experiences or opportunities like this in the past, those who have lived in different Canadian municipalities, and those with marginalized identities. Organizations were compensated $1,200 for their time and labour.

Organizations were chosen based on their geographical location, and expertise in each of the topical areas that the topics cover. This ensured that all 47 cities were covered, including Vancouver’s and Victoria’s neighboring cities. Priority was given to organizations that were both youth-led and youth serving, as well as those that served QTBIPOC communities.

The Pivot 2020 Index Data Collection & Verification Process

In fall 2020, 1,100+ Pivot 2020 team members started collecting indicators for the Pivot 2020 Index. The team had 2 weeks to gather this secondary data. To do so, indicators were assigned to each team. Some teams collected data on a city they were not as familiar with because each of our 47 cities varied in size. The data collection process continued with our 20+ data analysts to ensure the completion of the index.


About Vancouver & Victoria

It is important to represent the local reality and experiences of youth in their cities. Pivot 2020 also acknowledges that Provincial governments set municipal boundaries. Most provinces have amalgamated cities over the past two decades. BC did not. For THE GRID, Vancouver and Victoria were treated uniquely. Data was collected in all the municipalities that make up the Census Metropolitan Area (CMA) for each city. That data is represented separately in THE GRID. To allow for better comparisons, the project also chose to merge the data from each CMA to form a data point for Metro Vancouver and Metro Victoria. The following rules were used to merge data. These are noted in THE GRID.

Merge rule options:

  • Sum of all municipal totals
  • Average of Yes/No
  • Average
  • Weighted average
  • Minimum value across all municipalities
  • Maximum value across all municipalities

This is why the Pivot 2020 Index represents 47 cities.

All indicators went through a data verification process that was collected to ensure accuracy and quality. All indicators were submitted along with a reliable source. Pivot 2020 team members, data analysts, and a dedicated data review team reviewed each data point against the sources and corrected data accordingly, this could have included replacing a data source completely to ensure quality and accuracy.

Once data is checked it needs to be normalized to accurately compare each city:

  • Where necessary, data is converted to metric units
  • All cost-based data is normalized to US dollars, measured against the average value of the local currency, January 1st to December 31, 2020
  • Since Pivot 2020 was motivated by a desire to measure cities from the perspective of youth, and since reliable average youth income data does not exist in the majority of the cities in our Index, the project measured all cost indicators relative to one hour of minimum wage labour in US dollars each city. For example: If the minimum wage in a city is $10 and the cost of a movie ticket is $12, the cost of a movie ticket, tied to minimum wage equals 1.2

While reliable, robust, and defensible data is available for the overwhelming majority of Pivot indicators across all cities, in a minority of cases (<5%) reliable data simply does not exist. Where data does not exist, it has been left blank if “zero” does not apply.

Pivot 2020 Survey Development Process

The Pivot 2020 survey is an anonymous, voluntary research tool that was designed to better understand how young people (15-29 years-old) interact with their cities, what city topics help young people live, work, play and thrive, and where specific cities are strong or weak with regards to these topics. The purpose of using this quantitative data is to inform cities on how best respond to - and recover from - the COVID-19 pandemic.


Survey Design

The origin of the Pivot 2020 survey is from a global initiative built by Youthful Cities to include youth voices in civic spaces -- it was created by young people across 40 cities around the world.

Once Pivot 2020 began, the project’s Research Director and Equity Lead were responsible for adapting the 2016 Youthful Cities survey to reflect the project's needs and goals. They also incorporated feedback from the project’s Executive Director as well as a group of young women of colour representing various community based organizations and post-secondary institutions in the Vancouver area.


Research Ethics Process

As a commitment to research ethics, Pivot 2020 underwent an extensive review process by Simon Fraser University’s Research Ethics Board (REB), an independent committee established by the university Senate. As a post-secondary institution that receives funding from Canada’s three federal research agencies 1, they are required to follow the agencies’ Tri-Council Policy Statement: Ethical Conduct for Research Involving Humans (TCPS 2). As part of this, all post-secondary institutions are required to set up an internal review process to make sure that the human research they conduct aligns with the TCPS 2.

This review process included the submission of a Study Detail document that outlined the research questions, tools, collection and other relevant information. A template for the Study Detail document can be found on the University’s Office of Research Ethics website.

Additional documents were submitted individually to support the Study Detail. These documents included (but are not limited to) informed consent forms, all research questions and the technology used to support the data collection. When changes were made to the research an amendment was submitted for approval.

In November 2020, Simon Fraser University’s REB approved the final version of the survey (V5.0) for distribution. The final survey questionnaire is available here.

1 The Canadian Institutes of Health Research (CIHR), the Natural Sciences and Engineering Research Council of Canada (NSERC), and the Social Sciences and Humanities Research Council of Canada (SSHRC).


Survey Iterations & Revisions

To ensure relevancy of questions and topical areas in the survey, the Pivot 2020 survey went through various iterations and revisions. This process involved including various youth employed by the project throughout the fall.

The overall feedback was that the survey needed to be more inclusive and accessible, especially with respect to capturing the specific lived experiences of Black, Indigenous, and People of Colour. Concerns regarding the research ethics aspect of the survey, particularly regarding the lack of compensation for respondents were raised.

In October 2020, youth that were employed by the project were given an opportunity to review and revise the survey in more depth.

Based on the suggested changes the Equity Lead, along with a research advisor with experience in community-based research with Indigenous populations, made revisions accordingly. This revised survey was shared back with “testing triads” of youth from across Pivot 2020’s geographic regions and the Equity, Diversity, and Inclusion (EDI) team of approximately 17 young people employed by the project, for further feedback and suggestions.

The project’s Equity Lead revised the survey questions according to the feedback from the triads. This version was presented at a meeting of the EDI team for final review. It should be noted that there was not a full consensus within the EDI team about the relevance and accessibility of the survey to youth from marginalized and disenfranchised communities. However, they accepted a compromise that would allow for a more in-depth exploration of youth experiences during the interview stage of Pivot 2020.

The Equity Lead incorporated the remaining edits and suggestions from the EDI team into the final survey (Version 5.0). Some of the final changes, included:

  • Revised demographic characteristics to better capture the lived experiences of potential participants (e.g. expanded self-identification with regards to gender)
  • Added questions to capture opinions from marginalized youth about their experiences (e.g. racism, transphobia, ableism, conflict with police, etc.)
  • Added questions that take a deeper dive on some of the city topics that have become more relevant since COVID-19
  • Added an open ended question to the survey to provide the opportunity to answer “why” and share additional context to given responses

Since this version included some questions that may have elevated the risk to participants (e.g. there were questions about police interactions and racism, etc.), a research ethics amendment was submitted to Simon Fraser University’s REB. Valid questions were raised around compensation for taking the survey, specifically around arranging individual remuneration, which Pivot 2020 reviewed against research ethics and through consultation with experts at Simon Fraser University. In the winter, the project decided not to move forward with individual remuneration as a strategy for compensation in order to uphold the fundamental research requirement of voluntary participation.


Survey Distribution

Pivot 2020 used a strategy called snowball sampling. Project team members were offered the opportunity to complete the survey and distribute it to friends and organizational contacts if they chose. This was voluntary. The project’s communications team also used social media promotions to target harder to reach populations.


Data Clearing, Weighting & Tabulations

All survey data was exported out of SurveyMonkey and reviewed in SPSS. The first step was removing individual survey responses deemed invalid from the dataset:

Reason Total Removed
Speeding: completing the survey faster than 30% of the median survey time 5
Gibberish open ended comments 3
Outside of target age range 152

The research team then considered if partial surveys should be included; that is, the responses from individuals who started, but did not complete the survey. A review of the partially completed surveys revealed a higher drop-off rate among respondents completing the survey in French, likely stemming from several programming differences when compared to the English version of the survey:

  • The questions are arranged over 8 pages for the English survey vs. over 45 pages for the French survey
  • The French version was often missing the “I choose not to answer” response option on demographic questions (e.g.:§ respondents completing the survey in French who did not wish to share information about themselves would be required to drop out of the survey, whereas their English counterparts would have been able to select “I prefer not to answer” and then continue)

No other systemic differences between those who completed the survey and those who dropped out part way were detected.

The research team therefore decided to include partially completed surveys if the respondent completed the upfront demographic section, up to and including question 16, and answered at least one attitudinal question. The requirement was set at question 19a or 19b, as questions 17-19 were optional and 19a/b had the largest number of responses.

Once the final dataset had been identified, the sample was reviewed to assess its reflectiveness of urban youth in Canada to determine if any weighting was required. Critical to decision making was knowing that the goal of Pivot 2020 was to hear from youth whose voices aren’t typically included in research, or who are often under-represented when they are included. In other words, the goal was not to have a perfectly representative sample.

If the sample was corrected by typical variables such as age or gender, Pivot 2020 would be diminishing or silencing the voices of exactly who the project wanted to hear from.

Leaning into diversity, the project decided to only weigh the data geographically when looking at the total survey sample to make sure the cities have the correct bearing on the total. This ensures the data is representative on the national level, and is important for ensuring THE GRID is useful. Please refer to the table below for more details regarding regional weighting.

Pivot 2020 Interview Development Process

The Pivot 2020 interviews are meant to center youth stories in their own voice. While quantitative data shares a glimpse of what is happening, it is through qualitative data that one can feel why it matters. The interviews were meant to complement the survey and index data by going in-depth on the issues that matter to youth and uncover the ‘why’ behind the quantitative data.


Interview Design

The research team designed and refined the interview prompts with the objective of using the interview as an in-depth exploration of the survey results. Specifically, the interview was designed as a semi-structured discussion to help the project understand how youth’s interactions with their city changed since the beginning of the pandemic, why certain issues or topics are the way they are and what cities could become. The interviews aim to identify gaps, challenges and opportunities related to the city’s COVID-19 crisis response and recovery.


Ethics, Confidentiality & Privacy

Participation in the Pivot 2020 interviews was entirely voluntary and no compensation was offered to the interviewees. Both the interviewee and the interviewer had the option of terminating the interview at any time. All interviews were confidential, meaning that no name can be linked to the extracted quotes. Moreover, each interviewee had the option of choosing whether they wanted to be recorded. In either case, all interviewees had to give their informed consent to the interview. Once the interview was completed, the interviewees received a copy of the recording or written notes for their review and were given two days to request any changes or their removal from the project. For more information, please read through the Pivot Interview Consent Form here.

The interview process was approved by the SFU Research Ethics Board (REB) in September 2020. It was amended in November 2020 to reflect a change in the technology used to conduct the interviews remotely.

In an effort to uphold the highest standards of privacy and security, Pivot 2020 decided to conduct the interviews via a custom video-conferencing technology, built on the SimpleWebRTC framework by a company called Talky, Inc. This allowed the project to conduct the interviews remotely, without data passing through any server -- making it highly secure and private. Upon completion of the interview, the recording was stored directly on a server located in Canada via an encrypted connection.

To provide another layer of privacy and confidentiality, Pivot 2020 is only sharing excerpts from recorded interviews on THE GRID.


Interview Collection

In the fall of 2020, a cohort of Pivot 2020 team members were asked to conduct 1-3 interviews each. Given the time constraints and the desire expressed by the project’s members to center young people's voices in the process, the scope of the Pivot 2020 interviews was dedicated to hearing from youth (aged 18-29) about their city.

The interview could be performed with: 1) a Pivot 2020 peer (a team leader or team member from the Pivot 2020 network who lives in the same city as the interviewer); and/or 2) a youth outside the Pivot 2020 network (a young person whom they knew outside the Pivot 2020 network, who also lived in the same city).

As a result, 500+ interviews were conducted, each lasting approximately 30 minutes. From these, the Pivot 2020 fall cohort was tasked to perform thematic analysis that addressed: 1) how have people’s interactions with their city changed because of COVID-19?; 2) how do people imagine the future of their city and their place within it?; 3) what opportunities and barriers exist to help or hinder making that future a reality? The project hopes to curate some of this analysis in future iterations of THE GRID.


Interview Cleaning & Analysis

The fall program leaves a legacy of more than 20,000 minutes (350+ hours) of recorded interview time and associated transcript trend scanning. Audio recordings were automatically transcribed via Amazon Transcribe. However, we did not approve Amazon’s usage of the data beyond the transcription process or on servers outside of Canada. The transcripts were reviewed for accuracy, removal of personal identifiers, and expletives. The transcripts were meant to stay as close to the spoken language as possible, honouring youth ways of speaking and local expressions. They therefore include words such as ‘um’ and ‘like’.

After being checked for accuracy, the interviews were reviewed to identify themes. To ensure consistency across the many people conducting the thematic analysis, a list of topics and themes (the thematic dictionary) was created prior to analysis. The dictionary is based on the questions asked in the interviews and on a pre-set list of urban topics. Interviewee comments could be assigned to multiple themes; not all comments were assigned to a theme. Basic demographic information about the interviewee is included with their verbatim comment.

Once coding was completed, all copies of the recordings were destroyed.
